The Puzzle

Why do nearly identical point clouds cost so differently to encode?

Gorilla

● 2.24M points • Animal • KME=4.94

85 Mbps

NASA Rover

● 2.17M points • Vehicle • KME=0.91
Encoding cost at r5:
38 Mbps
Nearly identical size. Radically different cost.
Two sequences with virtually the same number of points require 2.2× different encoding bitrates. Point count alone cannot explain this gap — topology, articulation, and motion complexity are the true drivers.
Another paradox: Wardrobe (1.48M pts, barely moves: KME=0.04) encodes at 76 Mbps — more than Dancing Man (0.77M pts, full-body dance: KME=3.03) at just 18 Mbps. Dense static geometry is more expensive than motion.

The Myth: Point Count Predicts Bitrate

More points → more bits? Not quite. The scatter plot exposes a surprisingly weak link (R²=0.41) — topology and motion explain the rest.

Hover a point to explore

Each dot represents one sequence. The dashed regression line shows the weak linear trend between point count and bitrate.

Key finding: R²=0.41 means point count explains only 41% of the variance in encoding bitrate. Topology, articulation, and motion are the hidden drivers.

Content-Dependent Rate-Distortion Curves

PSNR P2P (dB) vs Bitrate for all 6 representative sequences across quality levels r1–r5. Toggle sequences to compare RD efficiency.

Windmill achieves 79.8 dB PSNR at 35 Mbps. Gorilla needs 85 Mbps for only 75.2 dB — a >2× bitrate penalty for lower quality. Wardrobe starts unusually high at r1 (51.5 dB) because its dense, static geometry packs with surprisingly good initial quality.

What Really Drives Bitrate?

Motion energy (KME) vs Bitrate at r5. Bubble size = point count. Four quadrants reveal distinct compression regimes — and two stunning outliers.

🔬

Topology > Motion

Wardrobe's dense geometry costs 76 Mbps with nearly zero motion (KME=0.04), more than Dancing Man's full-body dance at 18 Mbps.

⚡

Rigidity Saves Bits

Gyroscope spins rapidly (KME=3.52) but encodes at just 35 Mbps — rigid geometry packs atlas tiles efficiently despite high motion.

📐

Point Count is a Myth

R²=0.41 — point count alone explains less than half the variance in encoding bitrate. Structure and motion explain the rest.

Category-Level Quality Analysis

Average PSNR P2P (dB) and encoding bitrate across all 6 object categories at each quality level — confirming content-dependent behaviour holds at the category level.

Avg. PSNR P2P (dB) by Category

Avg. Encoding Bitrate (Mbps) by Category

Artifact category has the lowest PSNR across all quality levels — irregular topology makes faithful reconstruction hardest. People & Architecture reach highest PSNR at r5 (77 dB), indicating these content types are best served by V-PCC's atlas-based approach.

Dataset Contents

Encoded Bitstreams
V-PCC (TMC2) encoded .bin files for all 21 sequences × 5 quality levels = 105 bitstreams.
Quality Metrics
Per-frame and per-sequence PSNR P2P, PSNR D2, MSE, Hausdorff distance measurements.
Content Features
Point count statistics, KME (kinetic motion energy), centroid displacement, spatial coverage per sequence.
Raw PLY Files
Original uncompressed PLY point cloud frames (selected sequences) for benchmarking decoders.
